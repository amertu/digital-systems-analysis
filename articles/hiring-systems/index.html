The Structural Flaws in Modern Tech Hiring

Amer Alkojjeh
July 25, 2025
Abstract

Modern tech hiring fails not due to AI or a lack of talent, but because it’s driven by industrial-era logic unfit for knowledge work. Companies optimize for efficiency outsourcing decisions, automating assessments, and measuring success by funnel metrics. AI systems don’t fix this they reinforce it, scaling bias and masking poor judgment behind algorithmic objectivity. Hiring has become a mechanical process that filters out complexity and creativity. This article argues for reclaiming hiring as a strategic, human-led function focused on long-term value, not short-term throughput.

Introduction

test The persistent dysfunction in how tech hiring operates—particularly in Europe—reflects deeper structural issues beyond surface-level inefficiencies. While some attribute the decline in software engineering demand to automation and AI, this perspective overlooks a more fundamental problem: the flawed conceptual foundations of modern hiring systems.

Hiring as a Mechanical Process

Many companies treat hiring not as a strategic, core function but as a process to be modularized, outsourced, or automated. Decisions are often made without sufficient involvement from engineering leadership, which is brought in too late, if at all. Hiring processes are driven by funnel metrics like time-to-hire, pipeline conversion, and cost-per-hire, which prioritize throughput over thoughtful team building and long-term value creation (Muller, 2018; Ton, 2014).

Mechanistic Roots and Taylorist Logic

This fixation on efficiency and abstraction is not new. It reflects an outdated logic rooted in industrial-era principles of scientific management—what has been referred to as “digital Taylorism” (Mitev & Venters, 2021). These models, originally designed for repetitive factory work, have been adapted—often uncritically—to knowledge work contexts like software engineering. As a result, hiring systems prioritize standardization, predictability, and risk-aversion, even in roles that demand adaptability and creativity (Buchanan & Huczynski, 2019).

The Platformization of Hiring

Hiring in tech has increasingly been absorbed into platform-based systems that reduce complex candidate profiles into keyword filters and ranking scores. This shift furthers the detachment of labor from context, as companies rely on algorithmic systems that obscure value and introduce bias (Graeber, 2018; Gray & Suri, 2019; Jones, 2021). Referrals and internal networks become key gatekeepers, bypassing formal structures altogether—a process confirmed by LinkedIn’s own economic reports (Graph, 2020).

A System That Cannot Learn

The result is a hiring ecosystem that does not merely make poor choices—it fails to learn from them. Because it was not designed for reflective adaptation but for cost-efficient filtering, meaningful feedback loops are absent. The dominance of KPIs creates what Muller calls a “tyranny of metrics,” where data replaces judgment and visibility overrides understanding (Muller, 2018).

Conclusion

Modern tech hiring systems are not broken because of automation or a lack of qualified talent. They are failing by design—guided by legacy assumptions that reduce people to inputs, obscure strategic thinking, and prioritize process over purpose. Until hiring is reclaimed as a core human and organizational function, the system will continue to filter out not just bad fits, but often the best ones.
