Broken Hiring Systems

Introduction

test The persistent dysfunction in how tech hiring operates—particularly in Europe—reflects deeper structural issues beyond surface-level inefficiencies. While some attribute the decline in software engineering demand to automation and AI, this perspective overlooks a more fundamental problem: the flawed conceptual foundations of modern hiring systems.

Hiring as a Mechanical Process

Many companies treat hiring not as a strategic, core function but as a process to be modularized, outsourced, or automated. Decisions are often made without sufficient involvement from engineering leadership, which is brought in too late, if at all. Hiring processes are driven by funnel metrics like time-to-hire, pipeline conversion, and cost-per-hire, which prioritize throughput over thoughtful team building and long-term value creation (Muller 2018; ton2014goodjobs?).

Mechanistic Roots and Taylorist Logic

This fixation on efficiency and abstraction is not new. It reflects an outdated logic rooted in industrial-era principles of scientific management—what has been referred to as “digital Taylorism” (Mitev and Venters 2021). These models, originally designed for repetitive factory work, have been adapted—often uncritically—to knowledge work contexts like software engineering. As a result, hiring systems prioritize standardization, predictability, and risk-aversion, even in roles that demand adaptability and creativity (Buchanan and Huczynski 2019).

The Platformization of Hiring

Hiring in tech has increasingly been absorbed into platform-based systems that reduce complex candidate profiles into keyword filters and ranking scores. This shift furthers the detachment of labor from context, as companies rely on algorithmic systems that obscure value and introduce bias (Gray and Suri 2019; Jones 2021; Graeber 2018). Referrals and internal networks become key gatekeepers, bypassing formal structures altogether—a process confirmed by LinkedIn’s own economic reports (Graph 2020).

A System That Cannot Learn

The result is a hiring ecosystem that does not merely make poor choices—it fails to learn from them. Because it was not designed for reflective adaptation but for cost-efficient filtering, meaningful feedback loops are absent. The dominance of KPIs creates what Muller calls a “tyranny of metrics,” where data replaces judgment and visibility overrides understanding (Muller 2018).

Conclusion

Modern tech hiring systems are not broken because of automation or a lack of qualified talent. They are failing by design—guided by legacy assumptions that reduce people to inputs, obscure strategic thinking, and prioritize process over purpose. Until hiring is reclaimed as a core human and organizational function, the system will continue to filter out not just bad fits, but often the best ones.
